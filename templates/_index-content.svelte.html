Looking back at Minnesota's past presidential candidates

By Jeff Hargarten • Star Tribune

February 6, 2019

With Sen. Amy Klobuchar announcing her entry into an increasingly crowded 2020 Democratic presidential field this week, she joins the ranks of other Minnesotans who previously have competed in America's biggest political contest.

Past presidential candidates from Minnesota haven't fared very well, with only one ever emerging from the primaries with the nomination. While Minnesota historically counts two Vice Presidents among its native sons, its record at helping get its own into the Oval Office is less impressive.

Klobuchar is a very popular senator in Minnesota, as evidenced by her dominant election victories, including 2018's 60 percent vote share. How her local popularity translates to the national stage remains to be seen.

Here's how the past presidential candidacies of Minnesota's politicians have performed throughout time:

Eugene McCarthy

1968 Democratic Primary

After representing Minnesota in both the U.S. House of Representatives and the U.S. Senate, Eugene McCarthy ran for president five different times. McCarthy's most famous campaign was his attempt to primary President Lyndon B. Johnson in 1968 on an anti-Vietnam War platform.

The primary was thrown into chaos when Johnson ultimately declined to seek a second term, pitting McCarthy against fellow Minnesotan and U.S. Vice President, Hubert Humphrey, who won the nomination, despite McCarthy faring well in prior state contests.

McCarthy ran for president again in 1972 and 1992 as a Democrat, and as an independent in 1976 and 1998. He didn't get very far any of those races.

Hubert H. Humphrey

1968 General Election

Though born in South Dakota, Hubert Humphrey is among Minnesota's most well-known politicians for, among other things, helping found the Democratic Farmer-Labor party and serving as the state's U.S. Senator.

Despite not campaigning in the messy 1968 primary, then-Vice President Humphrey won that year's presidential nomination at the Democratic National Convention.

Minnesota is among the most reliably Democratic states in the union during presidential election years, with the state only voting for Republicans three times in its whole history. Though Humphrey carried his home state, he still lost in a landslide to Richard Nixon.

Walter Mondale

1984 General Election

After a long history in national politics, which included serving as Minnesota's attorney general and then U.S. Senator, Walter Mondale ultimately became Jimmy Carter's vice president before winning the Democratic nomination for president in 1984.

That year, Minnesota continued its long history of Democratic voting and was the only state (plus Washington D.C.) to vote for Mondale, who suffered a crushing loss in Ronald Reagan's reelection landslide.

Tim Pawlenty and Michelle Bachmann

2012 Republican Primary

Former Minnesota Gov. Tim Pawlenty declared his candidacy for the GOP presidential nomination in 2011, but dropped out less than three months later after finishing third in the Ames Straw Poll.

Former Minnesota Rep. Michelle Bachmann won that same straw poll with a quarter of the votes. Bachmann then finished sixth in the Iowa caucuses, prompting her to leave the race – though she remained on most ballots before Super Tuesday.

Both Bachmann and Pawlenty eventually endorsed Mitt Romney for the Republican nomination, which he won. Romney was later defeated by President Barack Obama in the general.
